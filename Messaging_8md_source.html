3 ## Max Size
4 
5 A sensible upper-bound for message payloads is 1400 bytes:
6 
7 1. Max Datagram (1472) - Aeron header (32) = 1440
8 2. Round-down to cache-line boundary: (1440 & ~63) = 1408
9 3. Subtract MPMC queue header (8): 1408 - 8 = 1400

11 ## UDP
12 
13 The User Datagram Protocol (UDP) is a message-oriented transport layer protocol. UDP is the most
14 common transport layer protocol used with multicast addressing.
15 
16 UDP is a simple, stateless protocol that is best suited to applications that can tolerate:
17 
18  - congested buffers and networks;
19  - duplicate messages;
20  - lost messages;
21  - messages received out-of-order.
22 
23 For these reasons, UDP is often referred to as an unreliable protocol.
24 
25 ### MTU
26 
27 Although UDP supports datagrams of up to 64K in length, packing them into a single Maximum
28 Transmission Unit (MTU) is highly desirable to avoid fragmentation and subsequent reassembly. By
29 default, Linux will return `EMSGSIZE` if a user attempts to send a datagram that exceeds the target
30 link's MTU.
31 
32 UDP adds 8 bytes to the 20 byte header used by the IP protocol:
33 
34 ```
35  0 7 8 15 16 23 24 31
36 +--------+--------+--------+--------+
37 | Source | Destination |
38 | Port | Port |
39 +--------+--------+--------+--------+
40 | | |
41 | Length | Checksum |
42 +--------+--------+--------+--------+
43 |
44 | Data Octets ...
45 +---------------- ...
46 ```
47 
48 The maximum size of a datagram that avoids fragmentation is, therefore, the MTU size minus 28 bytes.
49 This can be verified on a link with an MTU of 1500 bytes as follows:
50 
51  $ ping -M do -c 1 -s 1472 www.reactivemarkets.com

53 ## Reliable UDP
54 
55 UDP can be made more reliable by adding sequence numbers to the application protocol; receivers can
56 implement a receive window to detect duplicates and restore datagram order.
57 
58 Re-sending the last datagram at regular intervals may be used as a form of keep-alive and for
59 detecting tail-loss on low volume channels.
60 
61 When a sequence gap is detected, the receiver must use a suitable method for recovering lost
62 transmissions. Recovery can be achieved by sending a Negative Acknowledgements (NAKs) back upstream
63 to the sender. Care must be taken, however, to avoid re-request storms that further burden the
64 network and risk compounding any capacity-related issues.
65 
66 A solution often deployed by market-data feeds is to provide a separate recovery channel that
67 periodically broadcasts snapshots for late-join or recovery purposes. Snapshot and delta channels
68 use the same sequence numbers, so that they can be synchronised by downstream consumers. The main
69 advantage of this approach is that events continue to flow in a single direction at a predictable
70 rate.

72 ## TCP
73 
74 Several characteristics of the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) make it a more suitable for
75 reliable messaging than UDP:
76 
77  - Ordering
78  TCP uses sequence numbers to ensure that data is received by the consuming application in the
79  same order that it was sent. Receive windows are maintained by receivers for reassembling data
80  in the correct order.
81 
82 <!-- end list -->
83 
84  - Flow-control
85  the receiver's remaining window-size is communicated back to the sender, so that the sender can
86  limit the transmission rate to avoid overwhelming the consumer.
87 
88 <!-- end list -->
89 
90  - Recovery
91  sending applications will automatically retransmit data that has not been acknowledged by the
92  consumer within a certain time period.
93 
94 In addition, the connected nature of TCP streams and their APIs allow applications to perform
95 specific actions when streams are connected and disconnected.
96 
97 Disclaimer: these points are intended to highlight key reliability features only. (Consult your
98 favourite TCP reference for more advanced features, including TCP's network congestion avoidance
99 algorithms.)
100 
101 In summary, UDP is ideal for messages that:
102 
103  - fit within a single MTU;
104  - do not depend on their predecessors;
105  - may be received out-of-order;
106  - may occasionally be lost;
107  - use multicast addressing.
108 
109 Good candidates for UDP are application heartbeats and snapshot-based market-data feeds.
110 
111 TCP should generally be preferred otherwise.

113 ## Late-join
114 
115 When a client subscribes to a stream, depending on the type of data, it may need to synchronise with
116 the current "state of the world". This is normally achieved by replaying historical messages or by
117 sending a snapshot.
118 
119 A typical example would be a delta-based market-data feed, where clients must acquire the current
120 state of the order-book, on which subsequent deltas can be applied.
121 
122 Such problems are easily solved using a reliable, connection-oriented protocol like TCP:
123 
124  - the client establishes a connection;
125  - the server sends an initial snapshot of the order-book;
126  - the server sends a sequence of deltas to apply.
127 
128 ## Packing
129 
130 Packed data-structures are important for message encodings where smaller sizes require less
131 bandwidth, decrease transmit times, and help to avoid fragmentation when message-oriented protocols
132 such as UDP are used.
133 
134 This is often in contrast with design considerations for internal data-structures. Concurrent
135 data-structures such as Events in the Disruptor pattern, for example, are often padded to avoid
136 false sharing and to adhere with the single-writer principle.
